Garamond

— a loveletter.
Even in today's digital forms,
Garamond evokes the hand.

It puckers and bloats in delicate ways like ink swelling within paper fibers. Reading its beautifully imperfect letters feels like reading a Renaissance manuscript. It is elegant while never feeling overly ornate or showy.

the Creator

Garamond is a group of many old-style serif typefaces, named for sixteenth-century Parisian engraver Claude Garamond.

Class & Catagory

Old-Style, Serif
Letters with a relatively organic structure resembling handwriting with a pen.

a Distinctive Character

An e with a small eye and the bowl of the a,
which has a sharp hook upwards at top left.

Medium, Material

One of the most important principles behind every good piece of design is that the designer has to master their medium.

With any medium, whether pencil and paper, metal, or pixels, the designer has to work with strengths and limitations. Typography is the perfect vehicle with which to illustrate this concept throughout history. From the beginning, the forms of our letters have been influenced by the tools we used to create them.

“The best design explicitly acknowledges that you cannot disconnect the form from the material – the material informs the form.”

— Jonathan Ive
Lead Designer, Apple

Good Web Font

What makes a font good or bad for the web?

There’s the widely-known issue of availability of fonts on users' computers, which is why we use widely-available fonts, like Arial, Verdana and Georgia. But popular web fonts are such not just because of their availability, but because they are drawn with the screen’s limitations in mind.

Georgia has a huge advantage over Garamond on-screen because it was designed to be displayed as such from the beginning. It was designed by Matthew Carter for Microsoft in the mid-90’s. This is evident in the sharp serifs on Georgia, rather than the more subtly modeled ones on Garamond, which get blurred and illegible at smaller sizes.

Bad Web Font

Now there are some fairly feasible ways of using whatever fonts we want,
doesn’t mean you should use just any font.

The reason is that our display technology isn’t up to par with paper. Even a great classic like Garamond, anti-aliased at 12px height on a modern computer screen doesn’t look so good because it’s just made up of a bunch of blocks of color.

Now we’re starting to get some display technologies approaching the quality of paper.
— Maybe Garamond can make its comeback.

In the Wild

Garamond truly lives on paper.

It's unsurprising that you find it often in books. Some of the most popular books have been set in a Garamond, including all the Harry Potter novels. A few other notable uses include Abercrombie & Fitch’s logo and Apple’s Think Different campaign.
